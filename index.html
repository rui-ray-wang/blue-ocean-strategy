Blue Ocean Strategy — Concept Overview

W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne · INSEAD · Blue Ocean Strategy (2005, expanded 2015)

Core Idea

Value Innovation — the Cornerstone

Simultaneously pursue differentiation AND low cost — not a trade-off
Create new demand rather than fighting for existing customers
Make the competition irrelevant by redefining the market space
Leap in value for buyers + cost reduction for the firm
🌊

Blue Ocean Strategy

Create uncontested market space by making competition irrelevant. Align innovation with utility, price, and cost.
Contrast

Red Ocean (Conventional Strategy)

Compete in existing market space
Beat the competition head-on
Exploit existing demand — zero-sum
Choose between differentiation OR cost
Align activities with chosen strategic position

The Four Actions Framework (ERRC Grid)

The operational tool for creating a new value curve — breaks the value-cost trade-off by systematically questioning every competitive factor.

CREATE

The ERRC Grid forces you to act on all four dimensions simultaneously — eliminating and reducing lowers cost; raising and creating lift buyer value. Together, they break the differentiation–cost trade-off that defines red ocean competition.

✂️

Eliminate

"Which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated?"
→ Remove what buyers no longer value but firms still invest in.

C
Cirque du Soleil: Animals, star performers, multi-show arenas — traditional circus costs that added no unique value to adult audiences.
SW
Southwest Airlines: Meals, seat assignments, hub connections, interlining with other carriers.
YT
Yellow Tail Wine: Oenological terminology, aging quality, prestige imagery in marketing.
📉

Reduce

"Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?"
→ Over-delivery in areas customers don't care enough about adds cost without proportional value.

C
Cirque du Soleil: Fun and humor (present but not the main draw), thrill and danger (reduced relative to traditional circus).
SW
Southwest: Price vs. full-service carriers. Fares reduced to compete with driving, not just flying.
NW
Nintendo Wii: Processing power and graphics vs. Sony/Microsoft — reduced to what casual gamers actually needed.
📈

Raise

"Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?"
→ Elevate factors where the industry under-delivers relative to what buyers actually want.

C
Cirque du Soleil: Artistic quality and live music — dramatically above traditional circus levels.
SW
Southwest: Frequency of departures, speed vs. driving, friendliness of service.
YT
Yellow Tail: Ease of selection, sweetness/approachability, retail display prominence.
✨

Create

"Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?"
→ Introduce entirely new sources of value that expand the buyer experience beyond existing alternatives.

C
Cirque du Soleil: Theme and storyline, refined venue/environment, multiple productions — closer to theatre than circus.
NW
Nintendo Wii: Motion-control physical gameplay — created a completely new form of gaming experience.
YT
Yellow Tail: Kangaroo brand identity, ease of choice at retail, fun social occasion positioning.

💡 Key discipline: Most firms only raise and create — they skip eliminate and reduce, which is why their costs rise with every product launch. A true Blue Ocean move simultaneously reduces cost structure (via eliminate + reduce) while lifting buyer value (via raise + create). This is the source of value innovation's profitability, not just growth.

The Strategy Canvas — Two Classic Cases

A diagnostic framework: competing factors on the x-axis, offering level on the y-axis. Read where the blue curve diverges from the red — that gap is the blue ocean move.

① Focus
Invest heavily in a few factors — not across everything. Selective excellence beats diluted mediocrity.
② Divergence
The curve must look different — not a shifted copy of rivals. Divergence signals a genuine strategic shift.
③ Compelling Tagline
If you can't summarize the strategy in one sentence that non-customers find compelling, it isn't a blue ocean yet.

The Six Paths Framework

Six systematic ways to reconstruct market boundaries — each path challenges a conventional assumption that keeps firms trapped in red ocean competition.

01

Look Across Alternative Industries

Buyers choose between substitutes across different industries (e.g., cinema vs. restaurants for an evening out). Identify why they switch and what factors drive choice — then eliminate the trade-offs.

NetJets (private aviation): Combined speed of plane travel with flexibility of car ownership. Target customers who chose between commercial flying and driving — not just airline passengers.
02

Look Across Strategic Groups Within Industry

Most industries have strategic groups (e.g., luxury vs. budget hotels). Buyers often choose across groups — understand why, and build a cross-group value offering.

Curves (fitness): Combined simplicity of budget gyms with the targeted nature of women's-only premium fitness. Customers chose between home exercise and traditional gyms — Curves served both needs.
03

Look Across the Chain of Buyers

Industries define "the buyer" narrowly — but the chain includes purchasers, users, and influencers. Shifting focus to a different buyer group can unlock new value.

Novo Nordisk (insulin): Focused on patients (users) instead of just doctors (purchasers) — innovated the Novopen, a discreet self-injection device that transformed patient compliance.
04

Look Across Complementary Products & Services

Value is often created or destroyed in what happens before, during, and after product use. Broaden the solution by addressing the full buyer context.

NABI (children's tablet): Included curated educational apps and parental controls in the tablet price. Parents wanted a safe digital environment — not just hardware — and competitors ignored that context.
05

Look Across Functional or Emotional Appeal

Industries converge on competing either on price/function OR emotional connection/status. Shifting the orientation unlocks new demand from buyers who want the opposite appeal.

Swatch: Shifted watches from expensive emotional status symbols to affordable fashion accessories — functional price, emotional variety. QB House: Stripped emotional ritual from Japanese barbershops; purely functional 10-minute cut.
06

Look Across Time (External Trends)

Decisive trends are visible today — but most firms adapt reactively. Blue ocean creators participate in shaping the trend early, asking: if this trend completes its arc, how should our value offering change?

Apple (iTunes): In 2003, music piracy was the dominant trend. Apple asked what the trend's logical endpoint was — legal, convenient, per-song digital purchase — and created the iTunes Store before piracy fully destroyed the music industry's model.
How to apply: For each of the six paths, list what you find when you look through that lens at your industry. The goal is not to use all six — it is to find at least one that reveals a new market boundary your competitors have not crossed. Most successful blue oceans emerge from one or two paths applied deeply, not six paths applied superficially.

Classic Blue Ocean Examples

Four landmark cases — each illustrates value innovation applied across a different industry and competitive context.

🎪

Cirque du Soleil

Entertainment · Founded 1984 (Montreal)
Eliminated
  • Animal acts
  • Star performers
  • Multiple rings
  • Aisle concession sales
Reduced
  • Thrill & danger
  • Fun & humor (relative to circus)
Raised
  • Unique venue (tent)
  • Artistic & live music quality
Created
  • Theme & storyline
  • Refined environment
  • Multiple productions
Blue Ocean Move: Redefined circus as adult theatrical entertainment. Targeted theatre/arts audiences (non-customers of circus) who would pay premium ticket prices. By 2000, Cirque had achieved revenues that Ringling Bros. took over 100 years to reach. Key: adult audiences pay 5× more than children — eliminating kids' fare (animals, clowns) and raising adult appeal was simultaneously cost-saving and revenue-expanding.
✈️

Southwest Airlines

Aviation · Founded 1967 (Texas)
Eliminated
  • Meals & seat class
  • Seat assignments
  • Hub connections
  • Interlining
Reduced
  • Price vs. full-service
  • Comfort features
Raised
  • Departure frequency
  • Speed vs. driving
  • Service friendliness
Created
  • Point-to-point routing
  • Fun, casual brand
  • Car-competitive price
Blue Ocean Move: Competed against the car, not just other airlines — targeted non-flyers who drove between cities. By offering air travel at car-trip prices with better speed, Southwest unlocked a massive new demand pool. Today it remains one of the most consistently profitable U.S. airlines, while most full-service carriers have cycled through bankruptcy.
🎮

Nintendo Wii

Video Games · Launched 2006
Eliminated
  • DVD playback
  • HD graphics
  • Online services (initially)
Reduced
  • Processing power
  • Game complexity
  • Controller buttons
Raised
  • Ease of play
  • Fun for non-gamers
  • Physical engagement
Created
  • Motion controls
  • Family social gaming
  • Senior/casual market
Blue Ocean Move: While Sony and Microsoft competed on processing power and HD graphics (red ocean: same buyers, same factors), Nintendo targeted non-gamers — families, seniors, casual players. Motion controls made gaming intuitive for those who had never played. Wii outsold PS3 by 3:1 in its first two years. Wii Sports became the best-selling game of all time at that point.
🍷

[yellow tail] Wine

Beverages · Casella Wines · Launched 2001
Eliminated
  • Oenological terminology
  • Aging quality tiers
  • Prestige imagery
  • Complex range
Reduced
  • Wine complexity
  • Price vs. premium
  • SKU count
Raised
  • Ease of selection
  • Retail prominence
  • Store staff support
Created
  • Fun, approachable brand
  • Sweetness/fruity taste
  • Social occasion identity
Blue Ocean Move: Targeted beer and cocktail drinkers (non-customers of wine) — people intimidated by wine's complexity and snobbery. Within two years of U.S. launch, [yellow tail] became the fastest-growing imported wine in American history, selling 4.5 million cases by 2003. It competed not just with other wines, but with Budweiser and Coors.

Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean — Full Comparison

Two fundamentally different views of strategy — not simply aggressive vs. conservative, but different assumptions about where value comes from.

Dimension 🔴 Red Ocean Strategy 🌊 Blue Ocean Strategy
Market Space Compete in existing market space — defined by industry boundaries Create uncontested market space — boundaries are reconstructed
Competition Beat the competition — rivals are the benchmark Make competition irrelevant — the benchmark is the non-customer
Demand Exploit existing demand — fight for the same pool of buyers Create and capture new demand — tap non-customers and latent demand
Value–Cost Trade-off Break the value–cost trade-off: choose differentiation OR cost leadership (Porter) Break the value–cost trade-off: pursue differentiation AND low cost simultaneously
Strategic Alignment Align activities with chosen strategic position (differentiation or low cost) Align all activities with value innovation — both differentiation and low cost
Industry Structure Industry structure is given — firms position within it (Structure-Conduct-Performance) Industry structure is shapeable — firms reconstruct it (Reconstructionist view)
Growth Source Market share gain — one firm's gain is another's loss Market creation — total market expands, competition is less relevant
Key Tool Five Forces (Porter) — analyze competitive dynamics and find defensible position Strategy Canvas + ERRC Grid — visualize current state, design new value curve
Risk Profile Known risks — competitive imitation, price wars, margin erosion over time Different risks — market not materializing, imitation once validated, execution
Sustainability Position defended through barriers — switching costs, scale, brands Lead time advantage + brand equity + cost advantage during creation period; must renew

Relationship to Porter's Framework: Blue Ocean Strategy does not reject Porter — it extends it. Porter's Five Forces explains why red oceans become bloody over time (rivalry, substitutes, buyer power). BOS answers: what do you do when that logic runs its course? It proposes that the most powerful competitive move is to make Five Forces analysis temporarily irrelevant by creating a new space. In practice, most firms need both frameworks depending on the stage of their industry and market.
